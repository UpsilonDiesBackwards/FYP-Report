\section{Simulation Architecture}

The simulation is developed using C++ due to its high performance and stability, and memory management
which will be important for handling large particle counts and their calculations efficiently.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Object-orientated design:} Each particle will be considered it's own object. Handling it's own position, radius, and systems such as rendering.
    \item \textbf{Environment:} Development is performed in a Linux environment using the GCC compiler.
\end{itemize}

The simulation also uses Dear ImGui (commonly referred to as \textit{ImGui, imgui, or IMGUI}) to render a Graphical User Interface (GUI), this will provide the user statistics such as the number of particles, performance stats, alongside providing the user the ability to add or remove particles. (\cite{ocornut-imgui})

\subsection{Problem with Units \& Scales}

The first issue encountered was the ubiquitous application of the International Systemm of Units \textbf{SI} \autocite{nist_si_units}, which standardises the accepted use, measurement, and notation
of quantaties, including mass, weight, speed, and distance.

The use of SI Units by scientific literature creates a problem when translating equations to code. This is because many values, such as the virtually infinitesimal mass of a proton: 
$1.67 \times 10^{-27}\,\mathrm{kg}$ become difficult to represent by a computer, often leaving to floating-point precision errors.

To avoid the imprecision of computational small numbers, we must normaliser the SI Units into a more manageable scale. By selecting a base reference, such as the mass of a proton, we can define
a new coordinate system where all other values are represented relative to that base reference.

For the simulation, the mass of a Proton $(m_{\proton})$ was selected as the fundemental unit of measurement (1.000$su$). All other masses are converted by dividing their $SI$ value by $m_{\proton}$.

\subsection{Gravitational}

The gravitational force, stated by \quoteat{fayyazuddin2011}{2}, is a `force of attraction between two particles and is proportional to their gravitational charges, namely their masses".
This definition was very easy to understand, stating that the force is the how attracted two particles are given their mass.

However, when the implementation of the force began, I quickly ran into the first \textbf{Lexical} issue.
The paper noted that the strength of the gravitational force was:

\begin{equation}
    G_n = 1.3\times10 ^{-54} (GeV/m)^{-1}
\end{equation}
\begin{flushright}
    \cite[p.~2]{fayyazuddin2011}
\end{flushright}

The first half of this equation made sense: $G_n = 1.3\times10^{-54}$, but what was $(GeV/m)^{-1}$?
